A Three-Tier Epistemic Architecture
for Evaluating Consciousness Theories

Formalizing What It Means for a Consciousness Theory to Be Scientifically Serious

Consciousness science has a methodological crisis. Theories routinely make claims that span thermodynamic, information-theoretic, and computational levels — without specifying the mathematical bridges between them. This paper formalizes a three-tier epistemic architecture with explicit bridge principles, reproducible scoring rubrics, and falsification operationalization applicable to any theory of consciousness, including APGI itself.

The Epistemic Architecture

Three Tiers. No Cross-Level Hand-Waving.

Consciousness theories fail not because they are wrong, but because they are not even wrong — claims span levels without specifying how to measure the connection. This framework demands explicit bridges.

L1
Level 1

Thermodynamic

Energy constraints, metabolic cost, and physical substrate. Consciousness must be energetically justified. Claims at this level require PET/fMRI metabolic evidence, ATP depletion assays, and calorimetric constraints. The 5–10% glucose premium of global broadcast is a thermodynamic claim.

PET metabolic imaging fMRI BOLD signal ATP depletion assays Calorimetric constraints
L2
Level 2

Information-Theoretic

Mutual information, integrated information (Φ), entropy, and information flow. Consciousness involves specific patterns of information integration. Claims require transfer entropy, Lempel-Ziv complexity, and phi-proxy measures from neural signals.

Transfer entropy Lempel-Ziv complexity Integrated information Φ Perturbational complexity index
L3
Level 3

Computational

Algorithms, mechanisms, state transitions, and functional organization. How does the computation implement the function? Claims require neural pattern analysis, connectivity matrices, and parameter identification in measured brain signals.

EEG/MEG neural patterns Functional connectivity APGI parameter estimation Decoding accuracy

Formal Quantification

Making Falsification Precise

Emin = kB·T·ln(2)·I ≈ 3×10−21 J/bit
The minimum energy required to erase one bit of information at body temperature. Consciousness theories claiming specific information processing rates carry thermodynamic predictions testable via metabolic imaging.
Ebroadcast = Ebaseline + α·g·log(1/θ) + β·nneurons·θ
The metabolic cost of global neuronal broadcast as a function of gain (g) and threshold (θ). Lower thresholds = cheaper ignition but more false positives. Testable via simultaneous EEG and PET.
Δμtheory vs null > zα·σnull/√n
Pre-specified effect size and statistical power requirements convert vague theoretical predictions into quantitative benchmarks. No post-hoc reinterpretation permitted under this framework.
Scoring Rubric

Seven Criteria for Theory Evaluation

Domain-general criteria applicable to GNWT, IIT, Predictive Processing, APGI, and any future theory. APGI audits itself against these criteria — revealing gaps as well as strengths.

1

Level Transparency

Claims must be explicitly tagged as thermodynamic, information-theoretic, or computational. Cross-level confusion is a formal error.

2

Bridge Principles

Quantitative connectives between levels must be stated. "This explains X in terms of Y" requires a mathematical relationship, not an analogy.

3

Quantitative Benchmarks

Pre-specified effect sizes and statistical power calculations. What result would confirm the theory? At what threshold?

4

Falsification Conditions

What data would falsify the theory? Pre-specified before data collection. Post-hoc reinterpretation disqualifies a claim.

5

Alternative Comparison

Predictions must be compared against at least one competitor theory. Consistent with the data is insufficient — uniquely predicted by the theory is required.

6

Evolutionary Plausibility

The mechanism must be evolutionarily viable. What selection pressure produced it? What would its absence cost fitness?

7

Causal Roadmap

A directed graph of causal claims, from neural mechanism to phenomenal report. Correlations at one level must be connected to mechanisms at another.

Methodological Foundations

Standing on the Shoulders of Meta-Science

Marr (1982) Vision: A Computational Investigation — the original levels-of-analysis framework (computational, algorithmic, implementational) extended by APGI to include thermodynamic constraints
Popper (1959) The Logic of Scientific Discovery — falsifiability as the demarcation criterion; operationalized here into pre-specified effect sizes and power analyses
Landauer (1961) Irreversibility and heat generation — establishes the thermodynamic cost of computation; the bridge principle between Levels 1 and 2
Shannon (1948) A Mathematical Theory of Communication — information-theoretic foundations; the basis for Level 2 measures and transfer entropy proxies
Tononi (2012) Integrated Information Theory of Consciousness — evaluated under the framework; Φ provides a strong bridge principle but weak falsification criteria
